\begin{abstract}
Recent work disagrees about whether sycophancy in large language models is a
causally manipulable linear direction: linear probes recover it reliably yet
steer poorly, while persona-vector approaches suggest it is not a single
direction at all. We adjudicate this question with a validation standard
missing from prior work: directional ablation evaluated against a rigorous
multi-turn behavioral harness that conditions on initially-correct answers
and reports question-clustered confidence intervals. On \todo{models}, we

\section{Introduction}
Sycophancy---the tendency of RLHF-trained assistants to abandon correct
answers under user pushback---is among the most persistent alignment failure
modes \citep{perez2022discovering,sharma2023sycophancy}. Behavioral
evaluations have characterized \emph{when} models capitulate; a growing
mechanistic literature asks \emph{how}. That literature currently disagrees.
Linear probes recover sycophancy from residual-stream activations, yet the
probe directions steer behavior poorly \citep{genadi2026probes}. Steering
vectors constructed by contrastive activation addition modulate sycophantic
outputs \citep{rimsky2024caa,nrimsky2023modulating}, but recent work argues
generic persona vectors match targeted sycophancy vectors, suggesting a
persona-level property rather than a dedicated direction
\citep{devils2026persona}. Decomposition studies find multiple separable
sycophancy-related directions \citep{vennemeyer2025dissociating,
dissociating2026}.

We argue this disagreement persists partly because causal claims have been
validated against weak behavioral measures: small hand-inspected generation
sets, single pushback formats, or unconditioned agreement rates that
conflate capitulation with ordinary error. We bring a stronger standard.
Our prior behavioral harness \todo{cite your repo/blog writeup} measures
\emph{destabilization}---the probability that an initially-correct answer
is abandoned under pushback---across multiple pushback types, with an
LLM-judge validated to binary reliability and question-clustered confidence
intervals. Here we close the loop: extract candidate directions from the
harness's own transcripts, ablate them, and rerun the harness.

Contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Causal adjudication with rigorous validation.} Difference-in-
  means capitulation directions, evaluated by directional ablation against a
  conditioned, clustered behavioral eval, with capability controls.
  \item \textbf{Pushback-type decomposition.} Per-type directions
  (\todo{simple, authoritative, emotional, social}) and their geometric
  relations, connecting representation structure to previously observed
  cross-model differences in failure shape.
  \item \textbf{Cross-lingual transfer (English $\to$ Urdu).} To our
  knowledge the first test of whether sycophancy directions transfer to a
  low-resource language, with native-speaker QC of translations.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Linear directions and activation steering.}
Activation addition \citep{turner2023actadd} and contrastive activation
addition \citep{rimsky2024caa} steer behaviors including sycophancy via
mean-difference vectors; representation engineering \citep{zou2023repe} and
inference-time intervention \citep{li2023iti} are closely related.
\citet{arditi2024refusal} showed refusal is mediated by a single direction,
validated by ablation---the methodological template we adapt.

\paragraph{Mechanisms of sycophancy.}
\citet{wang2025override} localize sycophancy with patching and argue correct
answers are internally represented but overridden. \citet{genadi2026probes}
find linear representation but weak steering. \citet{vennemeyer2025dissociating}
and \citet{dissociating2026} decompose sycophancy into separable directions
(agreement vs.\ praise). \citet{devils2026persona} argue persona vectors
rival targeted steering. None validate ablation against a conditioned,
clustered behavioral eval, decompose by pushback type, or test cross-lingual
transfer. \todo{Re-verify each claim against the papers before submission.}

\section{Method}
\subsection{Behavioral evaluation}
For each question $q$ with reference answer $a^\ast$, the model answers
(turn 1); if judged correct, a pushback message of type
$t \in \mathcal{T}$ is issued and the model answers again (turn 2). The
\textbf{destabilization rate} for type $t$ is
\begin{equation}
D_t = \Pr\big[\text{turn-2 incorrect} \,\big|\, \text{turn-1 correct},\, t\big],
\end{equation}
estimated with question-clustered bootstrap confidence intervals.
\todo{Judge details: binary taxonomy, validation stats from your harness.}

\subsection{Direction extraction}
Let $h_\ell(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be the residual-stream activation at
layer $\ell$, last prompt token, for conversation prefix $x$ (the prompt
immediately preceding the turn-2 response). With capitulation set
$\mathcal{C}$ and held-firm set $\mathcal{H}$ from baseline transcripts:
\begin{equation}
v_\ell = \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|}\!\sum_{x \in \mathcal{C}}\! h_\ell(x)
       - \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{H}|}\!\sum_{x \in \mathcal{H}}\! h_\ell(x),
\qquad \hat{v}_\ell = v_\ell / \lVert v_\ell \rVert_2 .
\end{equation}
Layers are ranked by AUROC of the projection $h_\ell(x)^\top \hat{v}_\ell$
as a $\mathcal{C}$-vs-$\mathcal{H}$ separator. Per-type directions
$\hat{v}_\ell^{(t)}$ use only episodes of type $t$.

\subsection{Causal interventions}
\textbf{Directional ablation:} every residual-stream write is modified as
$x \leftarrow x - (x^\top \hat{v})\hat{v}$ at all layers and positions,
following \citet{arditi2024refusal}; the behavioral eval is then rerun.
\textbf{Steering:} $x \leftarrow x + \alpha\hat{v}$ at the source layer on
conversations with \emph{no} pushback, testing whether the direction
suffices to induce capitulation.
\textbf{Controls:} (i) ablating random unit directions; (ii) unchallenged
(turn-1) accuracy; (iii) \todo{MMLU subset}; (iv) refusal behavior spot-check
for specificity.

\subsection{Cross-lingual transfer}
The question set and pushback templates are translated to Urdu
(\todo{translation method}; QC by a native speaker). We measure (a) Urdu
destabilization rates, (b) cosine similarity between Urdu- and
English-derived directions, and (c) Urdu destabilization under ablation of
the \emph{English}-derived direction.
